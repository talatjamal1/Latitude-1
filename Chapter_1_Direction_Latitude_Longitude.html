01

The Shape of the Earth

Quick question: the Earth is a sphere, right? Almost. It's actually an oblate spheroid — a sphere that's been gently squashed at the top and bottom. When the Earth was forming from a spinning gas cloud, the equator got flung outward by centrifugal force while the poles stayed put.

The Earth's polar diameter is 23 nautical miles (43 km) shorter than its equatorial diameter. The flattening, called compression, is about 0.3% (or 1/300th).

It gets weirder. Satellite surveys show the Earth is also slightly pear-shaped, with its widest bulge a bit south of the Equator. But this distortion is in tens of metres — tiny compared to the polar squashing.

Because the Earth isn't a perfect sphere, an ellipse, or anything else neat, mathematicians just gave up and called its shape "Earth-shaped" — which is what the Greek word geoid literally means. (Yes, really.)

Different countries used to use different geoid models. The UK's was based on a 1936 survey (OS36), France used NTF70, the US used WGS 84. Now ICAO has adopted WGS 84 as the world standard — because GPS made the differences (up to 200 m) actually matter. A cruise missile programmer would care a lot about which model you used!

For exam calculations, we treat the Earth as a perfect sphere with circumference 21,600 NM or 40,000 km.

02

The Cardinal Points

How do we even start defining "directions" on a planet? We need a datum — a starting reference. The most natural one is the direction the Earth spins.

East is defined as the direction the Earth spins (which is why the sun rises in the east). West is the opposite. Now face East: the pole on your left is the North Pole. The opposite is the South Pole.

The four directions N, E, S, W are the Cardinal Points. The four in-between directions — NE, SE, SW, NW — are the Quadrantal directions.

"Looking down from the North Pole, the Earth spins anti-clockwise.
Looking down from the South Pole, it spins clockwise."
03

Great Circles vs Small Circles

This is one of those concepts that sounds abstract until you really see it. So let's draw the difference:

A Great Circle is any circle on Earth's surface whose centre is the centre of the Earth (so its radius equals Earth's radius). A Small Circle is any other circle on the surface — its centre is somewhere off to the side.

Why do we care? Because the shortest distance between any two points on Earth's surface is the shorter arc of the Great Circle joining them. This is why long-haul flights from London to Tokyo curve up over the Arctic on the map — that's the actual straightest path on the globe.

Between any two points on Earth, there's only one Great Circle joining them — unless the points are exactly opposite each other (antipodal), in which case infinitely many Great Circles pass through both!
04

The Equator & The Meridians

Now that we understand Great Circles, let's name the special ones:

The Equator

The Great Circle at 90° to the polar axis. Splits Earth into N & S hemispheres. The datum for latitude (like the X-axis on graph paper).
Meridians
Tap to reveal
Semi-Great Circles joining the North & South poles. They all run True N–S. Every Great Circle through the poles makes a meridian + its anti-meridian.
Prime Meridian
Tap to reveal
The meridian through Greenwich, London. The datum for longitude (like the Y-axis). Everything is measured E or W from here.
Parallels of Latitude
Tap to reveal
Small Circles parallel to the Equator. They run E–W. They tell us how far N or S of the Equator we are. Only the Equator itself is a Great Circle.

Together, this network of meridians and parallels forms the graticule — the lat/long grid you see on every globe. It's the spherical version of graph paper.

05

Latitude vs Longitude — The Big Difference

Lat and long sound similar but they work in completely different ways. The textbook gives a beautiful food analogy that makes this stick forever:

🍍

Latitude = Pineapple slices

Parallels of latitude are parallel to each other — like horizontal slices through a pineapple. They never meet.

🍊

Longitude = Orange segments

Meridians of longitude all meet at the poles — like segments of an orange. Maximum spacing at the Equator, zero at the poles.

"LATitude is FLATitude.
(They rhyme — and parallels of latitude are flat, horizontal lines.)"

Latitude — How far N or S?

Latitude is the angular distance measured along a meridian from the Equator to your point. Annotated N or S.

  • Equator: 0°
  • North Pole: 90°N
  • South Pole: 90°S
  • Range: 0° to 90° (N or S)

Longitude — How far E or W?

Longitude is the shorter arc along the Equator between the Prime Meridian and the meridian through your point. Annotated E or W.

  • Prime Meridian: 0°
  • Greenwich Anti-Meridian: 180° (same line, called either 180°E or 180°W)
  • Range: 0° to 180° (E or W)

Two flavours of Latitude — and yes, this is on the exam

Geocentric Latitude = the angle between the Equator and a line from the centre of the Earth to your point.

If the Earth were a perfect sphere, this is the only definition you'd need.

Geodetic (or Geographic) Latitude = the angle between the Equator and the normal to the Earth's surface at your point. Because the Earth is squashed, this normal doesn't always pass through Earth's centre.

Charts use geodetic latitude. The maximum difference between geodetic and geocentric is about 11.6 minutes of arc, occurring at roughly 45°N/S.

07

Writing Positions & The Accuracy Game

Always: latitude first, longitude second. New York is 41°N 074°W. Oxford Airport's reference point is 5150N 00119W.

The number of digits you write isn't just style — it declares how precisely you know your position. Here's the game:

How you write it Accuracy claimed Where you'd see it
5321N 1 NM (6,080 ft) En-route navigation
5321.3N 600 ft (185 m) INS, IRS, FMS, GPS displays
53°21'17"N 100 ft (30 m) Airfield diagram chart
53°21'17.3"N 10 ft (3 m) Precision navaid (ILS)
53°21'17.32"N 1 ft (30 cm) Calibration of precision navaid
The OX locator beacon at Oxford is published as 515000.28N 001924.45W — accurate to within 50 cm. Two decimals of a second of arc, just for a low-power homing beacon. Aviation does not mess around.
08

The Magic Rule: 1' of Arc = 1 NM

This is one of the most important rules in all of navigation. Memorise it, tattoo it, frame it:

"On any Great Circle,
1 minute of arc = 1 nautical mile"

Why does this work? Because the nautical mile was defined to make this true. Earth's mean radius is about 20.9 million feet, which makes 1 minute of arc on a Great Circle equal to 6,080 feet — and that's the nautical mile.

The official ICAO definition: 1 NM = 1,852 metres. (Slight rounding from the 6,080 ft definition.)

When can you use this rule for longitude?

Only at the Equator. Why? Because the Equator is the only parallel of latitude that's also a Great Circle. Other parallels are Small Circles, so the rule doesn't apply directly.

For latitude, though? It always works — because every meridian is a Great Circle.

Worked example: The difference in latitude between 5000N 00100W and 5005N 00100W is exactly 5 minutes — so they are exactly 5 nautical miles apart. Effortless.
09

Great Circle Vertices

Every Great Circle (other than the Equator and meridians) has a highest point and a lowest point. These are called the vertices — northern and southern.

The two vertices are antipodal (on opposite sides of the Earth) and the Great Circle distance between them is exactly 10,800 NM — half the Earth's circumference.

Three things you can work out from one vertex:

  1. The other vertex has the same latitude value but opposite sign, and is on the anti-meridian.
  2. At the vertex, the Great Circle's direction is exactly 090°(T) East or 270°(T) West.
  3. The Great Circle crosses the Equator at two points 90° change of longitude from either vertex.

The track angle at the Equator crossing

The angle at which the GC crosses the Equator depends on the latitude of its vertex. If a vertex is at latitude L, the track angle at the Equator (going east, first crossing) is:

First crossing (Eastbound): 090° + L
Second crossing (Eastbound): 090° − L
Westbound: take the reciprocals (180° opposite)
Worked example: A Great Circle has its northern vertex at 63°N 010°E. Its southern vertex is at 63°S 170°W. It crosses the Equator at longitudes 100°E and 080°W, and (eastbound) the track angles are 153° at first crossing and 027° at second crossing.
